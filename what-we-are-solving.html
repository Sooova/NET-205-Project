What We're Solving

Looking back - A brief history of the changing labour relations in the digital economy

Social Media and Influencers

In 2024, there were approximately 50 million people worldwide who classed themselves either as influencers or social media content creators (Trittin-Ulbrich & Glozer, 2024, p.1812). Content creators were able to monetise their content by encouraging social media users to follow them and through commercial collaborations. Content creators were required to invest a significant amount of time to build up their following base and relied on algorithms to push their content to their viewers. In 2024, there was a lack of transparency on how these algorithms worked which meant that the content creators’ income was beholden to the platforms. Content creators provided free labour to social media networking sites such as Instagram, Facebook and TikTok through user generated content and through the creation of data. Platforms profited and exploited this free labour by selling the data to other organisations for targeted advertising (Helmond, 2015, p.4). While few content creators were able to generate an adequate income for their social media content, many influencers worked either for free or were paid inadequately (Trittin-Ulbrich & Glozer, 2024, p.1812).

Social media platforms were able to exploit content creators by profiting from the free labour provided by content creators. Social media sites such as Instagram had approximately four different types of profitable content creators (Yazdanipoor et al., 2022, p.179). This included:

Celebrities

Influencers

Shoutouts

Ordinary Users

Content creators such as Celebrities, Influencers and Shoutouts worked in collaboration with businesses to promote products or services. Ordinary users generated content on social media platforms which generated attention from other users. Social media platforms collected, monitored and sold the ordinary user’s data for advertising, which Advertisers used to target online advertisements (Dolber, 2016, p.747). Ordinary users provided the social networking sites with free labour by creating data, which was monitored and analysed for the platforms to monetize.

The Network Economy

Unseen unpaid labor

In the late 1990’s-2020’s, the Network Economy (also known as the Information Economy) thrived. The technology at this time enabled computers to connect through networks allowing “communication between computers” which enabled a way of creating wealth (Kelly, 1997, n.p.). Social Networking Sites such as Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, X (also known as Twitter) and TikTok became leaders in the field, creating a monopolisation as they purchased and merged other social networking sites with theirs. Users were required to create an account with these sites to be able to access other sites, e.g to sign up for Threads you needed to have an Instagram account. This gave content creators a limited number of options as they were required to either join these sites or to find another social networking site to remain connected. As the majority of content creators used one or more of these platforms, if a creator wanted to switch to another lesser-known social networking site it was likely that they would lose their followers. These platforms needed users and content creators to be successful and to profit. The more content and more user accounts that were connected to their networks, the more profitable these platforms were, this was known as a network effect (Srnicek, 2017, p.257). Content creators would have invested a significant amount of unpaid labour to develop their content and to form relationships/networks with their users, if they wanted to switch to another social network site this would take considerable time and resources to rebuild. Content creators were trapped into remaining with these social networking platforms.

The Power of Technological Companies

The Covid-19 Pandemic in 2020, highlighted the power of technological companies when they were able to switch their business models and infrastructure into other areas. During the pandemic, organisations and businesses which were not classed as essential services were closed, and the majority of employees were required to work from home. This was a major disrupter to the 2020 labour workforce, as organisations relied on digital technologies to provide services for employees to pivot to remote working models. Companies, organisations and the people depended on digital technologies to continue to work and socialise, through a time of physical isolation caused by Covid.

Technological companies were able to rapidly increase their services, as well as to expand into other areas such as healthcare models and education. For example, in the UK a private security company were able to transition monitoring and organising the booking and handling of Covid testing program, and Google and Apple partnered to develop a contract tracing solution; systems which would usually have been provided by professional health services (Solano et al., 2020, p.45). Covid brought a sense of urgency and uncertainty into the community which meant there was a decrease in the level of scrutiny, “with relaxed oversight and minimal contestation” for these organisations (Solano et al., 2020, p.45). During this time, five key trends were identified:

1

Public Sector Ownership

The integration of technology into systems which were usually owned by the public sector

2

Desensitization of privacy

A decrease in public sensitivity with privacy-invasive technologies

3

Political Inteference

Political empowerment to use infrastructure into new sectors

4

Regulatory Downfall

Decrease in regulations by the public sector

5

Increased Security

Increase in security technologies in areas which were not previously considered. (Solano et al., 2020, p.45)

The Covid-19 Pandemic highlighted the role and the reliance of digital technologies in our lives and the economy. During Covid-19, the relationship between technology and the digital economy may be considered as a time of neoliberalism, as private businesses provided services which typically would have been provided by government services, and economic growth was enabled through the decrease in oversight and regulations (Kammas, 2022, n.p.).
